## TL;DR

In a distributed system, remote calls fail in ways the caller cannot distinguish:
a request may be lost on the way out, or the response may be lost on the way back
after the work already happened. Reacting to that uncertainty naively creates two
new problems. Retrying an operation can apply it twice (duplicates), and many
clients retrying at once can overwhelm a recovering service (a retry storm). The
standard remedy is a small family of patterns that work together. Make operations
**idempotent** so a repeat is harmless, gate side effects with an **idempotency
key** plus server side deduplication, retry only what is **safe to retry**, space
retries with **exponential backoff and jitter**, bound every wait with a
**timeout**, stop hammering a failing dependency with a **circuit breaker**, and
collapse duplicate concurrent work with **single flight**. None of these is
optional folklore; each addresses a specific failure mode, and using one without
the others usually makes things worse.

## Why it exists

Networks fail **partially**. A healthy request/response has four points where it
can break: the request never leaves, the request arrives but the server dies
before finishing, the server finishes but the response is lost, or everything
succeeds. From the caller's seat, three of these four look identical, a timeout or
a dropped connection with no answer. Crucially, "no answer" does not mean "no
effect." The server may have charged the account, written the row, or sent the
message, and only the acknowledgment went missing. This is the ambiguity that
makes reliability hard: the safe looking reflex of "just try again" can silently
double an effect that already happened.

The second force is **correlated load**. When a shared dependency slows down or
briefly fails, every caller experiences it at the same moment and every caller
retries at the same moment. A service that was merely degraded now faces a
synchronized spike of duplicate traffic precisely when it has the least capacity
to absorb it. Retries **amplify** load: one logical request becomes three or four
physical requests, and if intermediate tiers each retry independently, the
amplification multiplies at every hop. A brief blip becomes a self sustaining
outage that persists after the original trigger is gone. These two forces,
partial failure and correlated retry load, are why the patterns below exist and
why they belong together.

## How it works

### Idempotency

An operation is **idempotent** when applying it two or more times has the same
effect as applying it once. This is a property of the operation, not of the
network. Reading a value is naturally idempotent. Setting a field to a fixed value
(`status = shipped`) is idempotent. Deleting a specific record by id is idempotent
(the second delete is a harmless no op). By contrast, "increment the counter by
one" and "append a line item" are **not** idempotent, because each application
changes the result.

In [[glossary#h|HTTP]] semantics the request methods `GET`, `PUT`, and `DELETE` are defined as
idempotent, and `GET`/`HEAD` are additionally safe (no intended side effect),
while `POST` is generally not idempotent. That distinction is the reason a browser
warns you before re-submitting a `POST` but happily re-issues a `GET`.

Where an operation is not naturally idempotent, you make a retry safe by pinning it
to an **idempotency key**: a unique token the client generates once per logical
intent and reuses on every retry of that same intent. The server records the key
together with the outcome. On a repeat, it recognizes the key and returns the
original stored result instead of performing the work again. This turns an
inherently unsafe operation ("create a payment") into one that can be retried
freely, because the second, third, and fourth attempts are **deduplicated** on the
server. Practical requirements for this to hold: the key must be generated by the
client (not the server, or a retry would mint a new one), it must be persisted
atomically with the side effect (so a crash between "do the work" and "record the
key" cannot lose the association), and it needs a defined retention window and
collision domain (scoped per account or per endpoint).

### Safe to retry vs unsafe

Before retrying anything, classify it. A retry is safe when the operation is
idempotent, or is protected by an idempotency key, or is a pure read. A retry is
unsafe when the operation has an un-deduplicated side effect and you cannot tell
whether the first attempt took effect. Also classify by **response**: retry on
transient signals (connection failures, timeouts, HTTP 429, HTTP 503, and other
5xx) but do **not** retry most 4xx client errors, because a malformed or
unauthorized request will fail identically every time and retrying only adds load.
The rule of thumb: retry transient, server side, idempotent conditions; do not
retry deterministic client errors.

### Retry with exponential backoff and jitter

![[retry-with-backoff.drawio.svg]]

When you do retry, **when** you retry matters as much as **whether**. Retrying
immediately, and at a fixed interval, is the recipe for a retry storm. Two
mechanisms fix this:

- **Exponential backoff.** Increase the wait between attempts multiplicatively,
  for example base delays of 1, 2, 4, 8 seconds, capped at some maximum. This
  gives a struggling dependency progressively more room to recover and bounds how
  aggressively any single client hammers it.
- **Jitter.** Backoff alone is not enough, because clients that failed together
  will also back off together and retry together, just on a slower synchronized
  clock. Jitter breaks that synchronization by randomizing each delay. The widely
  cited "full jitter" strategy picks each wait uniformly at random between zero and
  the current exponential cap, which spreads retries across the whole interval and
  gives the lowest server load. Variants ("equal jitter," "decorrelated jitter")
  trade a little more total time for a little less variance; full jitter is a sound
  default.

Additional guardrails on the retry loop itself: cap the **number** of attempts,
cap the **total** elapsed time (a retry budget), and only retry at **one** layer
of the stack. Retrying at every tier compounds multiplicatively, so pick a layer
and make the others fail fast. A client side **retry budget** (for example, allow
retries to add at most ten percent to total request volume) prevents retries from
becoming the dominant source of load during an incident.

### Timeouts

Every remote call needs a **timeout**; without one, a caller blocks indefinitely on
a hung dependency and eventually exhausts its own threads or connections, spreading
the failure upstream. Bound both connection establishment and the overall request.
Setting timeouts too short, though, is its own failure mode: it converts slow but
succeeding requests into failures, triggering retries that add load and can push a
merely slow service into an actual outage. Tune timeouts from observed latency
(for instance a high percentile of normal response time plus headroom), not from a
guess.

### Circuit breakers

A **circuit breaker** stops a caller from repeatedly calling a dependency that is
clearly failing. It tracks recent failures; once they cross a threshold it "opens"
and fails fast for a cool down period instead of sending doomed requests. After the
cool down it allows a few trial requests ("half open"); if they succeed it closes
and resumes normal traffic, otherwise it opens again. This gives the downstream
service breathing room to recover and preserves the caller's own resources
(threads, sockets) instead of tying them up on calls that will time out anyway. The
breaker complements retries: retries handle brief blips, the breaker handles a
sustained outage where continuing to retry is counterproductive.

### Single flight and request deduplication

**Single flight** collapses multiple concurrent, identical requests into one. If N
callers (or N threads) ask for the same key at the same moment, only one call goes
downstream and the rest wait for and share its result. This is especially valuable
in front of a cache during a "stampede," when a popular entry expires and every
request tries to recompute it simultaneously. Where idempotency keys deduplicate
retries **over time**, single flight deduplicates concurrent work **at an instant**.

### Where each belongs

- **Idempotency keys and server side dedup:** on write endpoints with side effects
  (payments, order creation, message sends).
- **Retries with backoff and jitter:** on the client side of any remote call,
  restricted to safe/idempotent operations and transient errors.
- **Timeouts:** on every remote call and cross process call, always.
- **Circuit breaker:** at the client, wrapping calls to a dependency that can be
  down for a sustained period.
- **Single flight:** in front of expensive or cacheable computations that many
  callers request at once.

## Trade offs & when to use

- **Idempotency keys** add storage and a lookup on the write path, plus a policy
  decision about retention and key scope. The cost is real but small, and for any
  operation that moves money or sends externally visible effects it is essentially
  mandatory.
- **Retries** trade latency and load for success rate. They genuinely rescue
  transient failures, but every retry is extra load, so they only pay off with
  backoff, jitter, caps, and a budget. Unbounded retries are worse than no retries.
- **Timeouts** trade a few false failures (slow requests cut off) for protection
  against unbounded blocking. Too generous and you hang; too tight and you
  manufacture failures. There is no universal number, only a number derived from
  your latency distribution.
- **Circuit breakers** trade some availability during recovery (they may reject
  requests that would have succeeded) for stability of the overall system. They
  shine for sustained outages and are less relevant for single transient errors,
  which retries handle better.
- **Single flight** trades a little coordination overhead and shared fate (all
  waiters share one result, including a failure) for a large reduction in
  duplicate work under contention.

Reach for the whole set when a call crosses a process or network boundary and its
failure or duplication would matter. Skip the heavy machinery for purely local,
in process calls, or for naturally idempotent reads where a bare timeout plus a
capped retry is sufficient.

## Pitfalls / done-right checklist

- [ ] Retrying **non idempotent** operations with no idempotency key. This silently
      creates duplicates (double charges, double sends). Fix the idempotency first,
      then enable retries.
- [ ] Backoff **without jitter.** Synchronized clients back off in lockstep and
      retry in lockstep; the storm just arrives on a slower clock. Always jitter.
- [ ] **No cap** on attempts or total time. A retry loop with no budget turns a
      transient blip into sustained self inflicted load.
- [ ] Retrying at **every layer** of the stack. Amplification multiplies per hop.
      Retry at one layer; make the rest fail fast.
- [ ] Retrying **4xx client errors.** A deterministic client error fails
      identically on every attempt; retrying only adds load.
- [ ] **Missing or infinite timeouts.** A call with no timeout can hang forever and
      exhaust the caller's threads and connections.
- [ ] Generating the idempotency key **server side**, or on each attempt. The key
      must be generated once by the client per logical intent and reused on retries.
- [ ] Recording the idempotency key **non atomically** with the side effect. A
      crash in the gap loses the association and reopens the duplicate window.
- [ ] Treating a **timeout as a definite failure.** Timeout means "unknown," the
      work may have completed. Design the retry to be safe under that ambiguity.
- [ ] No **client side throttling / retry budget.** Cap retries as a fraction of
      normal traffic so they cannot dominate load during an incident.

## Mental model

Think of a call to a remote service like ordering across a busy counter through a
courier who might get lost in either direction. If you never hear back, you cannot
tell whether the order was placed, so you attach a **claim ticket** (idempotency
key): the counter keeps a ledger and, if it already filled that ticket, it hands
back the same result rather than making a second one. When you retry, you do not
retry the instant you get nervous, you wait, and each wait is a little longer and a
little randomized (**backoff and jitter**) so that you and everyone else in the
crowd are not all shouting the same instant. You give up on any single attempt
after a fixed wait (**timeout**) rather than standing there forever. If the counter
is clearly closed, you stop walking up to it for a while (**circuit breaker**) and
check back periodically instead of queuing pointlessly. And if ten people next to
you want the exact same thing right now, one person orders and everyone shares it
(**single flight**). Idempotency makes a repeat harmless; everything else controls
how and how often the repeat happens.
